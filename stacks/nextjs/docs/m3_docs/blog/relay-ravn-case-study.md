# How Ravn solves design-developer handoff with Relay

> Source: https://m3.material.io/blog/relay-ravn-case-study

Posted by
 Louise Macfadyen , Senior Material Design Advocate
 Handing off design represents one of the most critical, and often most stressful, stages of a development project. The exchange of visual and interaction work from designers to developers requires detailed documentation and communication, and, as an inherently collaborative endeavor, asks teams to work together often and early.
 
Design handoff is a progression of steps throughout the design process, not a single moment in time. Given good communication between design and development, the process should be simple. However, many issues can crop up over the lifecycle of product development. Details can become lost, miscommunications can cause setbacks. Often, the sheer volume of questions to be answered can be overwhelming, especially in most organizations where there’s a mismatch between the number of designers and developers.

## Meet Ravn
 
[Ravn](https://www.ravn.co/) is a design and development agency distributed across the US, El Salvador, and Peru, and an early access partner using Relay to pass work from Figma into Jetpack Compose. The agency has an estimated 12 designers, compared to more than one hundred developers, and while this isn’t an uncommon state of affairs, it can present a challenge as the sheer volume of questions confirming design decisions becomes a strain on design teams.
 “I don’t want to overwhelm them,” says Mario Chois, a Mobile Engineer at Ravn. “Imagine that everyone is consulting the two designers related to the [project].. You have to attend the calls from all the developers… it [can be] upsetting.” 
Product Designer Lea Escobar agrees. The design team at Ravn had tried setting up workshops and shareouts, but found that they didn’t solve the problem. “There are a lot of hurdles,” she says. “We spend hours in those meetings and those conversations, and we don’t have enough time to work on the real challenges… it’s tough.”
 
In some ways, this hurdle can never disappear: design workflows are conversations. However, solving some basic issues frees up time to solve bigger problems: designers can pay more attention to their work, and developers are freed up to do more code. If great product design involves developers and designers collaborating to find great solutions to difficult problems, a product like Relay provides enormous value in creating more time in the day for these vital conversations.
 
Handoff across specialties can boil down to individual preference, or broad commercial standards. How details like interaction and styles get transferred are predicated on expectations set early in the process. Engineers might have a preference to how a specific component is annotated, but having a standardized structure can make things more efficient across the board.
 
Providing a consistent understanding of handoff standards and details like dynamic elements can work wonders - Relay’s detailed parameters provide for more rich messaging across handoff. Details around design intent, like interaction behaviors, can be hard to guess. “Designers do everything on a stop motion layout” says Mario, whereas the reality can be a lot more complicated.

## Enter Relay
 
Relay’s standardized component structure, with editable dynamic elements, provides an easy onboarding to new teammates and contributors, too. “It's funny”, says Lea, “because I think about the design and to me it's really obvious - you may have to change something, an image that might be dynamic, or content will be different in another state, but that's not apparent for everyone, especially if they're new to the project, or they haven't been involved in the process. So [communicating] those small details - it's really helpful.”
 
Within design systems, details like styles and tokens can support efficient product development across handoff, but only if they’re appropriately documented. Like a standardized component structure, using system tokens can help create a shared language across disciplines. Keeping a design system organized is very important to cohesion, and while the upfront time spent can be onerous, the overall result is improved efficiency.
 
“We're actually trying to define our documentation best practices right now” says Lea, “we're always thinking of ways to be more efficient, and also how to improve the whole communication part of [handoff].”
 
There are other efficiencies, too. “We are [currently] taking a philosophy of trying to use what is already done.” notes Mario, something Relay helps with. “For example, having all my components available on a pre-built configuration? It’s pretty cool.”
 
It’s been a learning experience overall, say Lea and Mario. Working in this industry means we’re constantly retooling, adjusting our workflows, learning ways to be more efficient. Lea tells me working with Relay has felt like uncovering a new approach, one that involves a more defined and detailed format. “I think that it's been a great learning experience” she says “which is really nice - you never stop learning.”
 
How does the Ravn team feel about the future of using Relay? ‘Promising’ says Mario. But “really exciting”. Getting inside access to the tool was great, but onboarding as documentation was being established was a challenge. “I think it was hard because we were beta testers” he says, “so we had to figure out a lot - but the next person won’t have to go through that”. “Excited”, says Lea “I'm very excited what's gonna come next, and for us to keep using it”.
